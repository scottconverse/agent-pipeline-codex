# Schematic Restraint — design philosophy for the landing page

A philosophy of technical drawing as visual ethics. The composition adopts the
grammar of process & instrumentation diagrams, of Otl Aicher's pictograms for
Munich '72, of the great editorial pages of *Domus* and *Casabella* — work
where ideas are drawn rather than illustrated, where the page itself behaves
as an instrument. The visual ancestor is the engineering schematic; the
disposition is editorial. The reader who knows the lineage feels its quiet
authority; the reader who does not simply experiences a page that respects
their attention.

Space carries argument. Generous margins are not the consequence of having
too little to say — they are the condition under which what is said can land.
The grid is felt rather than seen. Every element finds its position by
relationship to a hidden rule, never by accident. Composition is the result
of countless small adjustments rather than a single confident gesture, and
that patience is visible in the work.

Colour is rationed like a precious reagent. A page that uses many colours
signals nothing in particular; a page that uses three signals deliberation.
Black is the structure. A warm paper-cream is the medium that lets the
structure breathe. One accent — a deep ink blue — is reserved for the
moments of consequence, the points at which a human takes responsibility for
the work. The accent is never decorative. Where it appears, it means
something.

Line work is hair-thin and unapologetic. The diagram is the masterpiece
moment of the composition — drawn with the precision of a nineteenth-century
technical journal. Numbered nodes, captioned figures, a spine, tick marks
for the gates that punctuate the flow. The marks are small. The viewer
leans in. The reward for leaning in is that every element makes sense.

Type is a typesetter's work, not a designer's flourish. Display sizes are
quiet, never theatrical. Body type sits at a humble dimension, set with
care. Monospaced characters appear only where they must — for code, for
figure references, for the small markers that anchor the eye in a technical
text. Letterspacing is measured. Hierarchy is built through restraint, not
through size escalation. Capital letters appear rarely, and when they do,
they are tracked open like a small-caps inscription on a brass plate.

The work must look as though it was made slowly. Every alignment the result
of patient comparison. Every margin the product of considered judgement.
The composition rewards sustained viewing — small details revealed on the
second pass, the third — and never feels improvised. This is a master
craftsman's page. It does not flatter the reader. It respects them. The
final test is whether a thoughtful peer, encountering it for the first
time, would set it on their desk to read carefully — not flick past in a
browser tab. If the answer is anything other than yes, the work is not yet
finished.
